### 1. Most Likely Root Cause

The model's failure to brake adequately at the hard corner is most likely due to the **unbalanced weighting of the loss function**. The mean‑squared error (MSE) is applied equally across all outputs (steer, accel, brake) and all frames, which means the model receives the same penalty for a large error in brake as it does for a large error in acceleration or steering. Given the dataset statistics:

- **Brake values are much smaller in magnitude** (mean ≈ 0.042, std ≈ 0.183) compared to acceleration (mean ≈ 0.628, std ≈ 0.398).
- **Brake is rarely used** (only 6.4% of frames have brake > 0.05, and even then, the mean brake value on those frames is 0.661, indicating that when braking is needed, it is often at a higher magnitude than the typical brake values).
- **The loss treats all errors equally**, so even large acceleration errors (which are more common and have larger magnitudes) are not penalized as heavily as a small but incorrect brake command.

This imbalance leads the network to prioritize minimizing larger, more frequent errors (acceleration) over learning the nuanced, low‑magnitude brake commands required for tight corners.
